The cash flow statement tracks the movement of cash and cash equivalents into and out of a business over a period of time.

It helps assess liquidity and the company’s ability to meet financial obligations.

The cash flow statement consists of three main sections:

Operating Activities

Cash generated from core business operations.

Examples include:
• cash received from customers
• payments to suppliers
• employee salaries
• operating expenses

Investing Activities

Cash used for acquiring or selling long-term assets.

Examples include:
• purchasing equipment
• selling property
• buying securities
• selling investments

Financing Activities

Cash flows related to financing the business.

Examples include:
• issuing shares
• borrowing loans
• repaying debt
• paying dividends

Positive cash flow indicates that the business generates more cash than it spends.

Negative cash flow may indicate expansion investments or financial distress depending on the context.

Investors and analysts examine cash flow statements to evaluate:

• liquidity
• operational efficiency
• financial sustainability
